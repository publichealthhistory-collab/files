Episode 1: Health by the People

In 1751, the English artist William Hogarth published two engravings—Gin Lane and Beer Street—that have since become iconic images of the social and health anxieties surrounding alcohol consumption in eighteenth-century London.

Produced during the height of the so-called “gin craze,” the paired images present a stark contrast. Gin Lane depicts a community in collapse, marked by poverty, neglect, hunger and social disorder, while Beer Street presents a prosperous and orderly urban world supported by productive labour, good health and social stability.

This episode examines the engravings as an early form of public health communication. Rather than reading them simply as moral warnings about individual behaviour, the video situates Hogarth’s work within wider debates about poverty, labour, urbanisation and social order, and explores the political and economic conditions that fuelled the gin craze.

The episode asks a broader public health question that remains relevant today: how health problems are framed, who is blamed, and how moral narratives can obscure the structural conditions that produce ill-health in the first place.

Episode 2: The Sulabh International Museum of Toilets — Sanitation, History and Social Change

This episode visits the Sulabh International Museum of Toilets in Delhi, a unique institution that uses history, humour and design to explore the global development of sanitation over the past four and a half thousand years.

The museum presents toilets not simply as technical artefacts, but as deeply social objects shaped by culture, religion, inequality and power. Its collection ranges from early sanitation systems of the Indus Valley civilisation to medieval commodes, nineteenth-century furniture toilets and contemporary innovations.

The episode also explores the work of the Sulabh organisation, founded in 1970 by sociologist Bindeshwar Pathak, whose twin-pit pour-flush compost toilet system has played a major role in reducing open defecation and eliminating manual scavenging in India.

Rather than treating toilets as a marginal or taboo topic, the episode shows how sanitation sits at the heart of public health, social justice and environmental protection, and why historical perspective remains essential for addressing contemporary sanitation challenges.
